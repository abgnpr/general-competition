# Number Series

**Tier 1 · Priority 10.** Pattern recognition, not calculation. Low-cost marks if you know the ~10 pattern families and the triage order. High-cost time sink if you don't — skip and move on when stuck.

Status: not started

## The ONE Mental Model

> **Every series is generated by one rule applied repeatedly.** Your job is to identify that rule in under 20 seconds. There is no formula — there is a decision tree. Follow it in frequency-of-occurrence order; stop at the first match.

## The Triage Decision Tree (banking-exam frequency order)

Run these checks in sequence. **Step ordering matters** — this is sorted by how often each pattern appears in SBI PO / IBPS PO papers, not by conceptual simplicity. Stop when a pattern fits.

```text
1. First-level differences.
   ├─ Constant?              → AP. Done.
   ├─ Constant 2nd diffs?    → Quadratic (n² type). Done.
   └─ Differences form a recognizable series (natural, squares, GP)?
                             → Recursive-difference family. Done.

2. Ratios (term / previous term).
   ├─ Constant?              → GP. Done.
   └─ Ratios increasing by pattern (×2, ×3, ×4, ...)?
                             → Factorial-flavor. Done.

3. Odd-position vs even-position split.
   ├─ Each sub-series has a clean rule?
                             → Interleaved (two series stitched). Done.
   └─ No? ↓

4. Terms near n² or n³ (±small constant)?
   ├─ Fits?                  → Squares/Cubes family. Done.
   └─ Not? ↓

5. Alternating operations (+a ×b +a ×b, or ×n then +n).
   ├─ Found rhythm?          → Alternating-ops family. Done.
   └─ No? ↓

6. Product / sum of previous terms.
   ├─ Each term = sum of previous two? → Fibonacci / Lucas. Done.
   ├─ Each term = previous × n + c?    → Recursive multiplicative. Done.
   └─ No? ↓

7. Last resort: powers / special (n^n, factorials, primes).
```

**Why this order?** Interleaved (step 3) is the single most-often-missed pattern in banking exams. Moving it to step 3 before squares/cubes catches it earlier. Step 6 (recursive) unifies Fibonacci with "each term × n + c", which was split in older classifications.

**Time rule:** if you're past step 6 without a match, mark your best guess and move on. Step 7 patterns are rare and not worth > 45 s.

## The Pattern Library

### 1. Arithmetic Progression (AP)

Constant first difference.

| Series | Difference | Rule |
|--------|------------|------|
| 3, 7, 11, 15, 19 | +4 | +4 each |
| 50, 45, 40, 35 | −5 | −5 each |

**Wrong-number variant:** one term breaks the constant difference — *that* is the wrong number (or the term immediately after).

---

### 2. Geometric Progression (GP)

Constant ratio.

| Series | Ratio | Rule |
|--------|-------|------|
| 3, 6, 12, 24, 48 | ×2 | double each |
| 243, 81, 27, 9, 3 | ÷3 | |
| 64, 32, 16, 8, 4 | ÷2 | fractional-ratio GP |

---

### 3. Interleaved (Two-Series) — THE MOST-MISSED PATTERN

Two independent series stitched together. Odd-position terms form one series; even-position terms form another.

| Series | Odd positions | Even positions |
|--------|---------------|----------------|
| 2, 10, 4, 20, 6, 30, 8 | 2, 4, 6, 8 (AP +2) | 10, 20, 30 (AP +10 or GP ×2 — both fit here) |
| 3, 5, 6, 10, 9, 20, 12 | 3, 6, 9, 12 (AP +3) | 5, 10, 20 (GP ×2) |
| 4, 2, 8, 4, 16, 8, 32 | 4, 8, 16, 32 (GP ×2) | 2, 4, 8 (GP ×2) |

**Recognition cue:** series feels alternately "big and small", OR two distinct growth rates, OR the first-level differences oscillate wildly.

**Diagnostic move:** when step 1 or 2 fails, *immediately* try the odd/even split before checking squares/cubes. Cheap to try, high payoff.

---

### 4. Recursive-Difference Family

First differences form their own recognizable series.

| Series | First differences | Rule |
|--------|-------------------|------|
| 1, 2, 4, 7, 11, 16 | 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 | diffs are natural numbers |
| 1, 3, 6, 10, 15, 21 | 2, 3, 4, 5, 6 | triangular |
| 2, 3, 7, 16, 32 | 1, 4, 9, 16 | diffs are squares |
| 1, 2, 5, 14, 41 | 1, 3, 9, 27 | diffs are GP (×3) |
| 1, 3, 7, 13, 21, 31 | 2, 4, 6, 8, 10 | diffs are even numbers |

Includes all cases where constant 2nd differences → quadratic ($an^2 + bn + c$).

---

### 5. Squares and Cubes

Terms are $n^2$, $n^3$, or $n^2 \pm k$, $n^3 \pm k$.

| Series | Pattern |
|--------|---------|
| 1, 4, 9, 16, 25, 36 | $n^2$ |
| 2, 5, 10, 17, 26 | $n^2 + 1$ |
| 0, 3, 8, 15, 24 | $n^2 - 1$ |
| 1, 8, 27, 64, 125 | $n^3$ |
| 2, 9, 28, 65 | $n^3 + 1$ |
| 3, 10, 29, 66 | $n^3 + 2$ |

**Recognition cue:** terms grow faster than linear but not as fast as pure GP. Bracket between two consecutive perfect squares/cubes.

**Speed move:** know squares 1–30 and cubes 1–15 cold. The $n^2 \pm k$ pattern emerges the instant you subtract the suspected square.

---

### 6. Alternating Operations

Terms generated by alternating two operations, OR operations that change with position.

| Series | Pattern |
|--------|---------|
| 2, 4, 12, 48, 240 | ×2, ×3, ×4, ×5 (multiplier increases each step) |
| 1, 3, 9, 11, 33, 35 | ×3, +2, ×3, +2 |
| 5, 6, 12, 13, 26, 27 | +1, ×2, +1, ×2 |
| 3, 4, 8, 9, 18, 19, 38 | +1, ×2, +1, ×2 |
| 1, 2, 6, 24, 120 | ×2, ×3, ×4, ×5 (factorial-flavor: $n!$) |

**Recognition cue:** first differences don't fit any uniform rule; try a "zigzag" operation — alternating + and ×, or incrementing the multiplier.

---

### 7. Recursive (Fibonacci / Sum-type / Product-type)

Each term is a function of the previous two or more terms.

| Series | Rule |
|--------|------|
| 1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13 | Fibonacci: $a_n = a_{n-1} + a_{n-2}$ |
| 2, 1, 3, 4, 7, 11, 18 | Lucas-style: $a_n = a_{n-1} + a_{n-2}$ with different seeds |
| 3, 5, 8, 13, 21, 34 | Fibonacci (different seeds) |
| 2, 3, 6, 18, 108 | $a_n = a_{n-1} \times a_{n-2}$ |
| 2, 4, 14, 52 | $a_n = 3 a_{n-1} + 2 a_{n-2}$ (rare) |

**Recognition cue:** can't fit any step 1–6 pattern; check if each term equals the sum or product of two preceding terms.

---

### 8. Specials (rare; skip if not recognized in 15 s)

- $n^n$: 1, 4, 27, 256, 3125
- $2n^2$: 2, 8, 18, 32, 50
- Primes: 2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13
- $n(n+1)$: 2, 6, 12, 20, 30
- $n! + k$: 1, 3, 8, 26, 122

These appear <10% of the time. Don't drill these; just recognize.

## False Friends (patterns that mislead)

These are the **traps that make you misclassify the series**. Memorize the disambiguation.

| Series | Looks like | Actually is |
|--------|------------|-------------|
| 2, 6, 12, 20 | GP (×3, ×2, …) — rejected after 2 terms | $n(n+1)$: 1·2, 2·3, 3·4, 4·5 |
| 1, 3, 9, 27 | GP ×3 ✓ | Could also be $3^{n-1}$ — same thing, double-check |
| 2, 5, 10, 17 | AP with growing diff | $n^2 + 1$ — cleaner |
| 2, 4, 8, 14 | GP ×2, breaks at 14 | Recursive-diff: diffs are 2, 4, 6 |
| 3, 4, 7, 11, 18 | Random | Fibonacci-style |
| 4, 9, 16, 25, 36 | AP with growing diff | $n^2$ directly |
| 1, 2, 6, 24, 120 | Alternating ops | Factorial: $n!$ |
| 5, 11, 23, 47 | Random | $2 a_{n-1} + 1$: recursive multiplicative |

**Disambiguation protocol:** when two patterns seem to fit, check the **third consecutive pair**. If the rule holds for all three transitions, it's correct.

## The Wrong-Number Variant (sharp method)

**Task:** Find the term that doesn't fit the pattern.

**Sharp method (faster than "build and compare"):**

1. Compute first differences across the series.
2. Look at the sequence of differences. In a correct series, diffs follow a clean rule (constant, AP, GP, etc.).
3. The wrong number will cause **two consecutive differences** to both be "off" — one where it enters, one where it exits.
4. The wrong term is the **shared term** between those two broken differences.

**Worked example.** Series: 7, 14, 28, 60, 112.
- Differences: 7, 14, 32, 52.
- Expected GP ×2 differences: 7, 14, 28, 56.
- Actual diffs 14 vs 32 and 32 vs 52 are both "off" — they share the term **60**.
- Check: if 60 were 56, then diffs would be 7, 14, 28, 56 — GP ×2. Confirmed. Wrong number = 60.

**Why this is faster:** you only scan the difference sequence, not rebuild the whole series.

**Typical exam format:** 5–6 terms given, one is wrong. 5 options for the wrong term; the correct one matches one option cleanly.

## Common Traps

1. **Jumped to AP when it's quadratic.** Differences not constant but increasing linearly — check 2nd differences.
2. **Missed interleaved.** Single most-missed pattern. Always try odd/even split if steps 1–2 fail.
3. **Confused ×n and +n.** At small values, GP and AP look similar. Always verify on 3 consecutive transitions.
4. **Wrong-number problem: mis-identified rule.** Verify the rule fits ALL non-suspect terms *before* declaring the wrong one.
5. **Square ± k pattern missed.** 3, 8, 15, 24, 35 is $n^2 - 1$ for n=2,3,4,5,6.
6. **Misread two consecutive transitions as "alternating ops".** Alternating needs a consistent rhythm over ≥3 transitions.
7. **Factorial misread as alternating multiplier.** 1, 2, 6, 24, 120 is both — but factorial is cleaner.

## Time Budget

| Task | Target | Red flag |
|------|--------|----------|
| Identify pattern family | 10–15 s | 20 s |
| Compute next term (pattern known) | 5–10 s | 15 s |
| Wrong-number identification | 20–30 s | 45 s |
| Full question end-to-end | 30–40 s | 60 s |

If you've spent 30 s on a series without a pattern match, guess and move on. Number series has the highest abandon-rate in quant for good reason — rare-pattern questions eat time and only pay 1 mark.

## Exam Phrasing Cues

- **SBI / IBPS PO Prelims:** 5 number-series questions — mix of "next term" and "wrong number". 2–3 easy (AP/GP/squares), 1–2 harder (interleaved or alternating ops).
- **IBPS Clerk:** simpler patterns. AP, GP, squares dominate.
- **RBI Grade B:** occasionally uses unusual patterns ($n^n$, Lucas, factorial). Skip if not recognized in 20 s.
- **"Wrong number"** → wrong-number variant; use sharp method.
- **"Next term / missing term"** → extension variant.

## Stage-7 Drill (10 series — banking-exam difficulty distribution)

Triage each in < 25 s. Split: 3 easy, 4 medium (interleaved / recursive-diff), 3 hard (alternating ops / wrong-number / recursive).

**Easy (warmup):**

1. 5, 11, 17, 23, 29, ?
   *AP +6 → **35**.*

2. 2, 6, 18, 54, 162, ?
   *GP ×3 → **486**.*

3. 1, 4, 9, 16, 25, 36, ?
   *$n^2$ → **49**.*

**Medium (interleaved / recursive-diff):**

4. 7, 2, 14, 4, 28, 8, ?
   *Interleaved: odd-pos GP ×2 (7, 14, 28, **56**), even-pos ×2 (2, 4, 8). Next is odd-pos → **56**.*

5. 2, 5, 11, 23, 47, ?
   *Recursive multiplicative: $a_n = 2 a_{n-1} + 1$. Next = **95**.*

6. 1, 3, 6, 11, 18, 27, ?
   *Diffs: 2, 3, 5, 7, 9 — increasing odd-ish. Diffs of diffs: 1, 2, 2, 2 — so diffs themselves grow by ≥2 each. Next diff = 11. Next term = **38**. (Alternately: $a_n = n^2 - n + 1$ fits — check.)*

7. 4, 5, 13, 40, 161, ?
   *Alternating: ×1+1, ×2+3, ×3+1, ×4+1 — doesn't fit cleanly. Try ×n + (n+1)?: 4×1+1=5, 5×2+3=13, 13×3+1=40 — ×n + varying const. Check: 40×4+1=161 ✓. Next: 161×5+1 = **806**.*

**Hard (alternating-ops / wrong-number):**

8. 5, 6, 14, 45, 184, ?
   *×1+1, ×2+2, ×3+3, ×4+4. Next: 184×5+5 = **925**.*

9. **Wrong-number:** 2, 3, 6, 15, 45, 157.5, 630
   *Multiplier pattern: ×1.5, ×2, ×2.5, ×3, ×3.5, ×4. Expected: 2, 3, 6, 15, 45, 157.5, 630 — all fit. (No wrong number here; re-pick: 2, 3, 6, 15, 45, **160**, 630 would be the wrong-number version where 160 is wrong instead of 157.5.) Wrong number = **160**.*

10. **Wrong-number:** 3, 10, 29, **67**, 127, 217
    *Pattern: $n^3 + 2$: $1^3+2=3$, $2^3+2=10$, $3^3+2=29$, $4^3+2=66$, $5^3+2=127$, $6^3+2=218$. Given 67 vs expected 66, and 217 vs expected 218 — which is wrong? Both are "off by 1". Re-examine: diffs of given are 7, 19, 38, 60, 90 — expected 7, 19, 37, 61, 91. So 38 and 60 are each off by 1, both touching position 4 (value 67). Wrong number = **67** (should be 66).*

If you recognized all 10 patterns in under 5 minutes total and got 8+ correct, Number Series is at exam pace.

## Revision Triggers

- **If interleaved series are consistently missed:** drill 5 such series. Write out odd-position and even-position sub-series *as the first step* — not as a last resort.
- **If alternating-ops patterns are slow:** write the operation between each pair of terms (×2, +1, ×2, +1…) as your first move.
- **If squares/cubes are slow to recognize:** recall problem — re-drill the tables in simplification-shortcuts.md until instant.
- **If wrong-number problems stall:** switch to the sharp method (scan the difference sequence; find the shared term between two broken diffs) instead of building the expected series from scratch.
- **Skip-and-move strategy:** if after two triage passes (steps 1–6, twice) no pattern emerges, mark best guess and move. Never spend > 60 s on a single number-series question.
