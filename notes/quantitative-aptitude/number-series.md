# Number Series

**Tier 1 · Priority 10.** Pattern recognition, not calculation. Low-cost marks if you know the ~10 pattern families and the triage order. High-cost time sink if you don't — skip and move on when stuck.

Status: not started

## The ONE Mental Model

> **Every series is generated by one rule applied repeatedly.** Your job is to identify that rule in under 20 seconds. There is no formula — there is a decision tree. Follow it in order; stop at the first match.

## The Triage Decision Tree

Run these checks in sequence on any new series. Stop when a pattern fits.

```
1. Compute first-level differences (consecutive gaps).
   │
   ├─ Constant? → Arithmetic progression (AP). Done.
   │
   ├─ Themselves an AP (constant 2nd differences)? → Quadratic. Done.
   │
   └─ Not obvious? ↓

2. Compute ratios (each term ÷ previous).
   │
   ├─ Constant? → Geometric progression (GP). Done.
   │
   └─ Not constant? ↓

3. Check if terms are near perfect squares or cubes (±small constant). Done if yes.

4. Check if differences are themselves a recognizable series (squares, primes, powers of 2).

5. Split into odd-position and even-position sub-series. Check each independently.

6. Check for alternating operations (+a ×b +a ×b, or ×n then ×(n+1) etc.)

7. Last resort: difference of differences (2nd-level). Rarely needed beyond this.
```

The exam is designed so that one of steps 1–6 always works within 20 seconds. If you're past step 6 on a single question, move on — you're burning time.

## The Pattern Library

### 1. Arithmetic Progression (AP)

Constant first difference.

| Series | Difference | Rule |
|--------|------------|------|
| 3, 7, 11, 15, 19 | +4 | +4 each |
| 50, 45, 40, 35 | −5 | −5 each |
| 2, 5, 10, 17, 26 | +3, +5, +7, +9 | Differences are odd numbers |

**Wrong-number variant:** one term breaks the constant difference — that's the wrong number.

---

### 2. Geometric Progression (GP)

Constant ratio.

| Series | Ratio | Rule |
|--------|-------|------|
| 3, 6, 12, 24, 48 | ×2 | double each |
| 243, 81, 27, 9, 3 | ÷3 | divide by 3 |
| 5, 15, 45, 135 | ×3 | |

**Fractional ratios** also appear: 64, 32, 16, 8 (ratio 1/2).

---

### 3. Squares and Cubes

Terms are $n^2$, $n^3$, or $n^2 \pm k$, $n^3 \pm k$.

| Series | Pattern |
|--------|---------|
| 1, 4, 9, 16, 25, 36 | $n^2$ |
| 2, 5, 10, 17, 26 | $n^2 + 1$ |
| 0, 3, 8, 15, 24 | $n^2 - 1$ |
| 1, 8, 27, 64, 125 | $n^3$ |
| 2, 9, 28, 65 | $n^3 + 1$ |
| 0, 7, 26, 63 | $n^3 - 1$ |

**Recognition cue:** terms grow faster than linear but not as fast as pure GP. Bracket between two consecutive perfect squares/cubes.

---

### 4. Differences are a Recognizable Series

First differences form their own pattern (squares, primes, Fibonacci, powers of 2).

| Series | First differences | Rule |
|--------|-------------------|------|
| 1, 2, 5, 14, 42 | 1, 3, 9, 27 | differences are GP (×3) |
| 1, 2, 4, 7, 11, 16 | 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 | differences are natural numbers |
| 0, 1, 3, 6, 10, 15 | 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 | triangular numbers |
| 2, 3, 5, 8, 12, 17 | 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 | differences +1 each |
| 1, 3, 7, 13, 21, 31 | 2, 4, 6, 8, 10 | differences are even numbers |
| 2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13 | — | prime numbers themselves |

---

### 5. Two Interleaved Series (Alternate-Position)

Two independent series stitched together. Odd-position terms form one series; even-position terms form another.

| Series | Odd positions | Even positions |
|--------|---------------|----------------|
| 2, 10, 4, 20, 6, 30, 8 | 2, 4, 6, 8 (+2) | 10, 20, 30 (×10) |
| 3, 5, 6, 10, 9, 20, 12 | 3, 6, 9, 12 (+3) | 5, 10, 20 (×2) |

**Recognition cue:** the series feels alternately "big and small" or two distinct growth rates.

---

### 6. Alternating Operations

Terms are generated by alternating two different operations (+a, ×b or ×n, ×(n+1)).

| Series | Pattern |
|--------|---------|
| 2, 4, 12, 48, 240 | ×2, ×3, ×4, ×5 (multiplier increases) |
| 1, 3, 9, 11, 33, 35 | ×3, +2, ×3, +2, ×3 |
| 5, 6, 12, 13, 26, 27, 54 | +1, ×2, +1, ×2, +1, ×2 |
| 3, 4, 8, 9, 18, 19, 38 | +1, ×2, +1, ×2 |
| 1, 2, 6, 24, 120 | ×2, ×3, ×4, ×5 (factorial pattern) |

**Recognition cue:** differences don't fit any uniform rule; try a "zigzag" operation on the series.

---

### 7. Mixed / Combined Patterns

$n^2 + n$, $n^2 - n$, $n(n+1)$, Fibonacci-style (each term = sum of previous two), etc.

| Series | Pattern |
|--------|---------|
| 2, 6, 12, 20, 30 | $n(n+1)$ |
| 0, 2, 6, 12, 20 | $n(n-1)$ or $n^2 - n$ |
| 1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13 | Fibonacci |
| 1, 3, 4, 7, 11, 18 | Lucas (Fibonacci variant) |

---

### 8. Powers of 2 / Powers of Specific Bases

| Series | Pattern |
|--------|---------|
| 1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32 | $2^n$ |
| 3, 9, 27, 81 | $3^n$ |
| 1, 4, 27, 256, 3125 | $n^n$ |
| 2, 8, 18, 32, 50 | $2n^2$ |

## The Wrong-Number Variant

**Task:** Find the term that doesn't belong. One term in the series violates the pattern.

**Method:**
1. Build the expected series using the triage decision tree.
2. Compare expected vs given at each position.
3. The first position where they diverge is the wrong number.

**Watch out:** the wrong number may shift which pattern you identify. If step 1 of the triage "almost" fits but one term is off, suspect that off term is wrong. Verify by checking that removing it makes the rest fit perfectly.

**Typical exam format:** 5 terms given, one is wrong. Usually only one obvious answer option (the corrected value) matches.

## "Find the Next Term" Variant

**Task:** Series has 5–6 terms; find the 6th or 7th.

**Method:** identical to above — identify the pattern, extend it. No difference in technique.

## Common Traps (memorize)

1. **Jumped to AP when it's quadratic.** Differences are not constant but increasing linearly — check 2nd differences.
2. **Missed the two-series structure.** Odd/even split is the most commonly missed pattern. If nothing else fits after step 4, always try alternating positions.
3. **Confused ×n and +n.** At small values, a GP and AP can look similar for the first 2 terms. Always verify on 3 consecutive terms.
4. **Wrong-number problem: computed the pattern wrong.** The most common wrong-number error is mis-identifying the rule on the "good" terms and then flagging the wrong position. Verify the rule fits ALL non-suspect terms before declaring the wrong one.
5. **Square ± k pattern missed.** $3, 8, 15, 24, 35$ feels random until you see $2^2-1, 3^2-1, 4^2-1, 5^2-1, 6^2-1$. Always check $n^2 \pm k$ when terms grow roughly quadratically.

## Time Budget

| Task | Target | Red flag |
|------|--------|----------|
| Identify pattern | 10–15 s | 20 s |
| Compute next term (once pattern known) | 5–10 s | 15 s |
| Wrong-number identification | 20–30 s | 45 s |
| Full question end-to-end | 30–40 s | 60 s |

If you've spent 30 seconds on step 1 without a pattern, mark your best guess and move on. Number Series questions have the highest abandon-rate in quant for good reason — the series may be a rare pattern, and the time cost of brute-forcing is not worth it.

## Exam Phrasing Cues

- **SBI / IBPS PO Prelims**: 5 number series questions — mix of "next term" and "wrong number". Usually 2–3 easy (AP/GP/squares), 1–2 harder (interleaved or alternating ops).
- **IBPS Clerk**: simpler patterns. AP, GP, and squares dominate.
- **RBI Grade B**: occasionally uses unusual patterns ($n^n$, Lucas, factorial). Skip if not recognized in 20 s.
- **Keyword**: "wrong number" → wrong-number variant; "next term / missing term" → extension variant.

## Self-Test (10 series — triage each in under 20 s)

Identify the pattern family only; don't compute unless asked.

1. 4, 9, 16, 25, 36, ?  *(squares: $n^2$; next = 49)*
2. 2, 6, 18, 54, 162, ?  *(GP ×3; next = 486)*
3. 1, 3, 6, 10, 15, 21, ?  *(triangular; differences +2,+3,+4... next = 28)*
4. 2, 5, 10, 17, 26, ?  *($n^2+1$; next = 37)*
5. 3, 5, 9, 17, 33, ?  *(differences double: 2,4,8,16; next = 65)*
6. 5, 8, 6, 11, 7, 14, ?  *(two interleaved: odd pos +1 each; even pos +3; next odd-pos = 8)*
7. 2, 4, 12, 48, 240, ?  *($\times 2, \times 3, \times 4, \times 5$; next ×6 = 1440)*
8. 1, 8, **28**, 64, 125  *(wrong number: should be 27 = $3^3$)*
9. 7, 14, 28, **60**, 112  *(wrong number: should be 56, GP ×2)*
10. 0, 1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, ?  *(Fibonacci; next = 13)*

If you identified all 10 pattern families in under 4 minutes total, Number Series is done.

## Revision Triggers

- If interleaved series are missed repeatedly, drill 5 such series: write out odd-position and even-position sub-series explicitly.
- If "alternating operations" patterns are slow, practice writing the operation between each pair of terms (×2, +1, ×2, +1...) as a first step.
- If squares/cubes are slow to recognize, that's a recall problem — re-drill the tables in simplification-shortcuts.md until instant.
- Skip-and-move strategy: if after two triage passes (steps 1–6 twice) no pattern emerges, mark best guess and move. Never spend more than 60 s on a single number-series question.
