# Percentages

**Tier 1 · Priority 1 · The master key.** Unlocks P&L, SI/CI, Ratios, and most DI.

Status: Day 1 complete (2026-04-20)

## The ONE Mental Model

A percentage is a **multiplier**, not a number to add. `+20%` means `×1.2`. `−15%` means `×0.85`. Chain multipliers for any sequence of changes. If you catch yourself adding percentages, you are doing it wrong.

Everything else in this chapter is a corollary.

## Fraction ↔ % Table (memorize cold)

The single highest-ROI investment in quant. These numbers appear inside P&L, SI/CI, DI, Ratios — every Tier 1 chapter.

**Read this table three ways** — fraction form, mixed % (exact), decimal % (approx). The mixed form exposes the pattern; the decimal form is what appears in options. Train to flip between all three instantly.

**Usage under exam conditions:**

1. See **37.5%** → think `3/8` → multiply by N/8 × 3, not N × 0.375.
2. See **16.67%** → think `1/6` → divide by 6.
3. See **44.44%** → think `4/9` → multiply by 4, divide by 9.
4. If the question gives mixed form (e.g. **14 2/7 %**), the fraction is `1/7` — often a signal that the answer will be a clean multiple of 7.

## The Full Formula Set

Organized by purpose. Core four in **bold** — if pressed for time, these four solve most problems; the rest are fast lookups for exam-day.

### A. Basic conversion

| # | Formula | When |
|---|---------|------|
| **1** | **`x% of N = (x/100) × N`** | Any "% of" computation |
| 2 | `% of whole = (part/whole) × 100` | Find what % a part is |
| 3 | `part = (% / 100) × whole` | Find the actual value |
| 4 | `whole = part × (100 / %)` | Find original when part + % known |

### B. Change & multiplier

| # | Formula | When |
|---|---------|------|
| **5** | **`% change = (new − old) / old × 100`** | Any increase/decrease. Base is always `old`. |
| 6 | `fraction change = (new − old) / old` | Same as above, in ratio form |
| **7** | **Multiplier: up r% → `×(1 + r/100)`; down r% → `×(1 − r/100)`** | Convert every % into a multiplier before chaining |
| 8 | Reverse a change: `original = final / multiplier` | Work backward after a % change |

### C. Successive changes

| # | Formula | When |
|---|---------|------|
| **9** | **Two changes a%, b% (signed): net % = `a + b + (ab/100)`** | Fastest shortcut for 2 changes |
| 10 | n changes: `[(1 ± a/100)(1 ± b/100)… − 1] × 100` | 3+ changes — just multiply the factors |
| 11 | Equal ± R% (up then down, or vice versa): net = **−R²/100**% (always a loss) | Classic P&L / discount trap |
| 12 | r% repeated n times: `Final = Initial × (1 + r/100)ⁿ` | Population, appreciation, CI |

### D. Base change (A vs B comparisons)

| # | Formula | When |
|---|---------|------|
| 13 | A is r% more than B → B is `r/(100+r) × 100`% less than A | "More" flipped to "less" |
| 14 | A is r% less than B → B is `r/(100−r) × 100`% more than A | "Less" flipped to "more" |

### E. Tricks

| # | Formula | When |
|---|---------|------|
| 15 | `X% of Y = Y% of X` | Swap when one side is cleaner |
| 16 | Price ↑ r%, same money buys `r/(100+r) × 100`% less quantity | Consumption problems |
| 17 | Price ↓ r%, same money buys `r/(100−r) × 100`% more quantity | Consumption problems |
| 18 | Expenditure `E = Price × Quantity`: % change in E = % change in P + % change in Q + (product/100) | Formula 9 applied to E = P × Q |

## Base Change (the highest-trap sub-topic)

"A is r% more than B" and "B is r% less than A" are **not** the same r. Different bases.

**One derivation, done forever.** A is r% more than B → A = B(1 + r/100). So B/A = 100/(100 + r) → B is `r/(100+r) × 100`% less than A.

| A vs B | Conversion | Memorable case |
|--------|------------|----------------|
| A is **r% more** than B | B is `r/(100+r) × 100`% less than A | 25% more → 20% less (1/5 less) |
| A is **r% less** than B | B is `r/(100−r) × 100`% more than A | 20% less → 25% more (1/4 more) |

**The shortcut nobody tells you:** use the fraction table.

- 25% more = ×5/4 → reverse = ×4/5 = 20% less
- 20% less = ×4/5 → reverse = ×5/4 = 25% more
- 50% more = ×3/2 → reverse = ×2/3 = 33.33% less
- 100% more (doubled) → 50% less (halved)

Base change is just **flipping the fraction**.

## High-Yield Shortcuts

1. **`X% of Y = Y% of X`**. Example: 16% of 25 = 25% of 16 = 4. Free marks.
2. **Equal ± R%**: increase by R% then decrease by R% (or vice versa) → **net loss = R²/100**%. E.g., +10%, then −10% → net −1%. Used heavily in P&L and SI/CI.
3. **Reverse a % change**: `original = final / multiplier`. After a 25% increase, divide by 1.25 (= ×4/5). Don't subtract 25%.
4. **% → ratio**: 40% : 60% = 2 : 3. Most DI caselets are easier as ratios than percentages.
5. **Net shortcut for doubled/tripled**: +100% = ×2, +200% = ×3. "Increased by 150%" means ×2.5, **not** ×1.5.

## MCQ Tactics (exam-only, not textbook)

- **Assume base = 100.** When no number is given ("X's salary is 20% more than Y's…"), set the smaller base to 100. All percentages become absolute numbers. Works for ~30% of percentage MCQs.
- **Assume base = LCM** when multiple fractions are in play. E.g., if 1/4, 1/5, 1/6 appear, set total = 60.
- **Option-elimination by parity/unit digit.** If the answer must be `(x/100) × 250`, last digit of answer × 100 must be ≡ last digit of (x × 250). Eliminate mismatched options before computing.
- **Approximate aggressively in DI.** 48.7% ≈ 50% − 1% = 1/2 − 1/100. Compute 1/2 × N, subtract N/100. Saves 15 seconds per cell.
- **Ballpark check.** Before marking, sanity-check: a 20% change must shift a number by roughly 1/5. If your answer is wildly off, you mis-identified the base.
- **Two-step chain, no calculator:** convert both to fractions first. +25% then −20% = ×5/4 × 4/5 = **×1** (zero net change). Almost impossible to see via decimals under time pressure.

## Calculation Speed Hacks

- **Split the %**: 17% of 450 = 10% + 5% + 2% = 45 + 22.5 + 9 = 76.5. Never multiply 0.17 × 450 on paper.
- **×25 = ×100/4**, **×125 = ×1000/8**, **×75 = ×300/4**, **×50 = ×100/2**. E.g., 36 × 25 = 3600/4 = 900.
- **÷ 5 = × 2 then shift decimal**: 87 ÷ 5 = 174/10 = 17.4.
- **11% and 9% shortcuts**: 11% of N = N/10 + N/100. 9% of N = N/10 − N/100.
- **15% = 10% + half of 10%**: 15% of 240 = 24 + 12 = 36.
- **Decimal drift**: 12.5% of N = N/8. 37.5% = 3N/8. 87.5% = 7N/8. Never compute these with decimals.

## Common Traps (memorize — these are where marks leak)

1. **Wrong base.** "% change" is always over the *original*, never the new value. "% more/less than B" uses B as base.
2. **Adding percentages.** Only valid when bases are identical. Successive changes → multiply.
3. **"% point" vs "%"**. Interest rate goes 5% → 7%. That is +2 *percentage points*, but a **+40% increase in the rate**. DI and banking GK traps.
4. **"Increased by 150%" ≠ ×1.5.** It's ×2.5. "Increased to 150%" is ×1.5. Read the preposition.
5. **Base change asymmetry.** A 25% more than B does *not* mean B is 25% less than A (it's 20%).
6. **Mixing up "of" and "more than"**. "A is 30% of B" (A = 0.3B) vs "A is 30% more than B" (A = 1.3B).

### Trap Pairs (drill side-by-side until the distinction is reflex)

| Sounds similar | Correct reading |
|----------------|-----------------|
| "increased by 20%" | × 1.20 |
| "increased to 20%" | final = 20% of original = × 0.20 |
| "A is 20% of B" | A = 0.2 B |
| "A is 20% more than B" | A = 1.2 B |
| "A exceeds B by 20%" | A = 1.2 B (same as above) |
| "A is 20% less than B" | A = 0.8 B |
| "B is 20% less than A" | B = 0.8 A → A = 1.25 B (different!) |
| "Rate rose from 5% to 7%" | +2 percentage points, +40% in rate |
| "Rate rose by 7%" on 5% base | 5% × 1.07 = 5.35% (almost never the intended reading — flag and re-read) |

## Problem Patterns → Attack Plan

| Pattern | Attack |
|---------|--------|
| "Income up a%, expenditure up b%, savings change?" | Write savings = income − expenditure, apply multipliers to each, not to savings. |
| Population/bacteria/price for n years | `Final = Initial × (1 + r/100)ⁿ`. Same form as CI. |
| "Pass marks = 40%, scored X, failed by Y" | Pass = 0.4 × total. Total = (X + Y) / 0.4. |
| "Salary up 20%, then down 10%" | Chain: ×1.2 × 0.9 = ×1.08 → +8%. |
| "A spends 60%, saves ₹X" | Savings = 40% of income → income = X / 0.4. |
| "Price reduced by r%, how much more can buy with same money?" | New quantity factor = 1/(1 − r/100). Answer = `r/(100−r) × 100`% more. Base-change in disguise. |
| Exam marks with negative marking | Convert to multiplier on attempts: `net = correct − (1/4) × wrong`. |
| "A's income is 25% more than B's, B's is 20% less than C's. A vs C?" | Chain: A = 1.25B, B = 0.8C → A = 1.25 × 0.8 × C = C. A and C equal. |

## DI Integration Checklist

- **Pie charts**: whole = 360° or 100%. Convert slice → value by multiplication, not division.
- **Tables with YoY growth**: each year is previous × (1 + r/100). Don't add growth rates.
- **Caselets**: identify the base in each sentence before calculating.
- **"Over/under average by X%"**: average is base, apply multiplier.

| Word problem (1 unknown, 1 equation) | 45 s | 75 s |

## Exam Phrasing Cues (Indian banking/insurance)

- **SBI/IBPS PO**: heavy on "salary ratio" + "% savings" multi-step chains. Base change appears in 1–2 Qs per paper.
- **IBPS Clerk / SBI Clerk**: direct "% of N", fraction conversions, simple successive change. Speed matters more than depth.
- **RBI Grade B / NABARD / SEBI**: tougher DI caselets with % growth + base change combined. Less direct percentage Qs, more embedded.
- **LIC AAO**: data-heavy. % change YoY + comparison across categories. Fraction table is decisive.
- **Keyword → attack**:
  - "respectively" → ratio problem in disguise, treat as ratio × common multiplier
  - "originally / initially" → reverse-multiplier question (`original = final / mult`)
  - "what percent" → answer is `(small/big) × 100`, always convert via fraction table first
  - "how much more/less" → absolute number, not %, re-read the question
  - "in terms of percent" → confirms the answer is a %, not a value

## PYQ-Flavor Drill Problems

Solve untimed first, then re-time at 45 s each. Each maps to a recurring exam pattern.

1. **(SBI PO)** A's salary is 20% more than B's. B's salary is 25% less than C's. If A earns ₹36,000, find C's salary.
   *Setup: A = 1.2B, B = 0.75C → A = 0.9C → C = 40,000.*

2. **(IBPS PO)** The population of a town increases 10% in Year 1, 20% in Year 2, decreases 25% in Year 3. Net % change?
   *Chain: 1.10 × 1.20 × 0.75 = 0.99 → −1%.*

3. **(IBPS Clerk)** 35% of a number is 175 more than 20% of the same number. Find the number.
   *0.15N = 175 → N = 1166.67. (Likely the paper expects N = 1167 or reframes the question.)*

4. **(RBI Grade B)** A's income is ₹X. He spends 40% on rent, 25% of the remainder on food, saves the rest. If savings = ₹9,000, find X.
   *After rent: 0.6X. After food: 0.75 × 0.6X = 0.45X = 9000 → X = 20,000.*

5. **(NABARD)** If the price of sugar rises by 25%, by what percent must a family reduce consumption to keep expenditure unchanged?
   *Use Formula 16: 25/(100+25) × 100 = 20%.*

6. **(LIC AAO DI)** In 2022, company sales were ₹8,00,000. In 2023 sales grew 15%, in 2024 dropped 10%. Find 2024 sales.
   *8,00,000 × 1.15 × 0.90 = 8,28,000.*

7. **(SBI PO base-change trap)** A's marks are 50% more than B's. By what percent are B's marks less than A's?
   *50/(100+50) × 100 = 33.33%. (Not 50%.)*

8. **(IBPS Clerk calculation)** 62.5% of 480 = ?
   *5/8 × 480 = 300. (Fraction table; never decimal.)*

## Day-7 Self-Test (if you can't do these in 45s each, revisit)

1. Convert instantly: 37.5%, 62.5%, 44.44%, 41.67%, 28.57% → fractions.
2. A is 60% more than B. B is what % less than A? *(37.5%)*
3. Price ↑ 20%, ↓ 20%. Net? *(−4%)*
4. Salary ↑ 25%, ↑ 20%, ↓ 10%. Net? *(+35%)*
5. 64% of 175 *(use swap or fractions: 16/25 × 175 = 112)*
6. After +25%, price is ₹250. Original? *(200, via ÷1.25 = ×4/5)*
7. Expenditure ↑ 12%, price ↑ 20%. Consumption change? *(Consumption × price = expenditure → consumption = 1.12/1.20 = 0.933 → −6.67%)*
8. 5% of 80 + 80% of 5 = ? *(8. Same number twice.)*

If you can explain **why** each answer is right without the formula sheet, Percentages is done.
